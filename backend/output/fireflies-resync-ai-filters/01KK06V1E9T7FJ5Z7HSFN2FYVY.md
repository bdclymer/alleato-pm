## **Never Say Die Brand and Story**

The **Never Say Die bourbon brand** combines a unique heritage story with a high-quality product, setting a strong foundation for growth. (00:14)

- **Compelling brand origin story** centers on a horse born on a Kentucky farm in 1951, revived with bourbon after a near-death birth, then winning the 1954 Epsom Derby at 33-to-1 odds.  
    - The win broke a 70-year drought for American horses in that race, boosting Kentucky’s horse racing reputation.  
    - The story links to the Beatles’ origins through a woman who bet on the horse, funding the first Beatles music club in Liverpool.  
    - Brian Luftman emphasizes this story as the brand’s core marketing asset, with potential for viral or film adaptation.

- **Product quality and production** have been perfected with a top-tier distillery in Danville, KY, known for fermentation expertise.  
    - The whiskey is aged 7 years, with barrels shipped to England for a final year of aging to enhance flavor and quality.  
    - Specialty offerings like barrel picks and reserve brands aged 12 to 18 years are planned for launch within the year.  
    - The brand has received notable recognition, including being named the world’s best bourbon by Forbes in 2023.

- **Current inventory and valuation** include about **3,000 barrels** spanning vintages from **2017 to 2024**, with an estimated **$7.5 million liquidation value** including bottled products and inventory.  
    - This stock supports a production and sales volume consistent with a premium craft brand aiming to grow.  
    - Private debt of around **$3.5 million** exists, with plans for some debt conversion to equity to reduce financial risk.  
    - The company operates as a **C corporation** with all common shares for alignment, with founders and investors as shareholders.

- **Marketing and future storytelling potential** remains underdeveloped but promising.  
    - Limited marketing so far, mostly organic growth based on product quality and a few sponsorships like the Nashville Derby.  
    - Philip Johnson highlighted the opportunity to use Hollywood and YouTube-quality feature film style storytelling to amplify the brand.  
    - Brian advises timing marketing pushes to coincide with wider distribution to maximize impact and avoid early spikes without shelf presence.

## **Distillery and Retail Experience Development**

Building physical venues is central to the growth strategy, focusing on tourism, brand engagement, and controlling customer experience. (00:21)

- **Downtown Lexington satellite distillery** planned as a tourism and retail hub, distinct from the main distillery which is outsourced in Danville.  
    - The facility will feature a bar, tasting rooms, and retail space, targeting **June-July opening for bar** and **August 15th for full tourism experience**.  
    - The building is under a ground lease with an option to purchase by **summer 2029**, with a **$700,000 deposit** on the lease and a total buildout budget of **$3 million**.  
    - The city approved zoning changes last summer enabling distilleries downtown; occupancy limits and fire safety measures are planned with options to expand capacity later.

- **Main distillery buildout** requires a **4,000 to 6,000 square foot** industrial space, estimated at around **$1 million** for equipment and engineering.  
    - Shane Baker, a fermentation expert and mechanical engineer, will assist with design and cost planning.  
    - Barrel warehousing will be off-site for optimal aging conditions in central Kentucky, as climate affects whiskey quality significantly.

- **Retail strategy pivots from distribution-only to experience-driven sales**, aiming to drive traffic to owned venues.  
    - Brian stressed that controlling where customers spend money is more profitable than just wholesale distribution.  
    - Plans include exclusive barrel bottlings only available at distillery locations to build brand loyalty and premium sales.  
    - Locations considered include high foot-traffic tourist towns like New Orleans, Nashville, Savannah, and Michigan’s top market Grand Rapids.

- **Tax incentives and financing** support buildout economics.  
    - A **$1 million tax credit** from the Kentucky tourism board will rebate up to **$100,000 annually for 10 years**, aiding cash flow.  
    - Additional construction sales tax rebates may be available for material-heavy projects, enhancing financial viability.  
    - Private loans and investors are converting debt to equity, reducing interest costs and aligning interests.

## **Partnerships and Expansion Opportunities**

Collaborative ventures and geographic expansion are positioned to leverage collective resources and market presence. (00:42)

- **Partnership structure emphasizes equity alignment**, with all investors sharing in the **C corp** ownership and dilution, fostering unified goals.

    - Debt levels and dilution are monitored closely, with openness to new investors to reduce financial risk.

## **Operational and Legal Foundations**

Strong operational groundwork and regulatory approvals underpin the brand’s growth roadmap. (00:38)

- **Building ownership and lease arrangements** are structured for flexibility and financial prudence.  
    - The Lexington site is acquired via a ground lease with a low-interest loan at **3%**, expiring in **summer 2029**, allowing phased investment and an option to purchase.  
    - The lease includes no personal guarantees, with the building as collateral, reducing investor risk.  
    - The team is confident about meeting all local historic preservation and zoning requirements, focusing on exterior facade compliance only.

- **Licensing and permits** are well advanced to accelerate opening timelines.  
    - Federal TTB permits are secured; liquor licenses will be obtained post-inspection during buildout.  
    - City council and fire code offices support the distillery project, allowing a max of **50 people** at the upstairs tourism area, with plans for future fire escape upgrades to expand capacity.  
    - Prior experience with historic projects in Lexington supports smooth regulatory navigation.

- **Tax credits and rebates** provide financial advantages.  
    - The Kentucky tourism board tax credit applies to the Lexington buildout but excludes bar sales revenue, affecting the total rebate amount.  
    - Potential construction sales tax rebates exist for material-heavy projects, which may benefit partner sites like Union where material costs dominate.  
    - Coordination with consultants is planned to maximize these incentives and shorten cash flow cycles.

- **Corporate governance and shareholder alignment** focus on simplicity and unity.  
    - All investors hold common shares in the C corp, avoiding class distinctions to maintain aligned incentives.  
    - The board includes founders and a co-founder from Castle and Key Distillery, adding industry expertise.  
    - This structure reduces conflicts between brand management and venue operations, integrating investments under a single equity umbrella.
